Year City Was Incorporated

Established in the 1730s, the city underwent several name changes, including New Carthage, New London, and Newton, before settling on its current name, Wilmington, in 1740. The city was named in honor of Spencer Compton, the 1st Earl of Wilmington.

Region of the State

Wilmington serves as the eastern terminus of Interstate 40, a transcontinental freeway stretching 2,554 miles to Barstow, California, where it connects with I-15, the gateway to Southern California. Along its route, I-40 traverses numerous major cities and state capitals. According to data from the United States Census Bureau, he city covers a total area of 52.97 square miles (137.2 km2), with 51.41 square miles (133.2 km2) being land and 1.56 square miles (4.0 km2) (2.95%) consisting of water. Notably, Wrightsville Beach is a popular destination in the Wilmington area, while Carolina Beach and Kure Beach further enhance the city's allure.

Average Income Compared to the Rest of the State

In 2022, the median household income in Wilmington, NC, stood at $63,835, reflecting a 5.7% decrease from the state-wide median annual income of $67,481 in North Carolina. However, compared to the median income of $31,099 in 2000, this signifies a notable increase of 51.3%. Similarly, the per capita income in 2022 amounted to $43,229, indicating a 50.3% increase from its 2000 counterpart of $21,503.

Extending over nearly two miles, the Riverwalk in Wilmington transcends its role as a mere picturesque pathway. Stemming from a vision born in the 1980s to rejuvenate Wilmington's historic downtown, it has evolved into the vibrant heart of the city. Adorned with eateries offering al fresco dining, quaint boutique shops, and frequent events, the Riverwalk embodies Wilmington's lively ambiance. Moreover, it offers uninterrupted views of the scenic Cape Fear River, enhancing its appeal as an essential destination within the city.
